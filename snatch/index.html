The Story

Diamonds, dogs,
and absolute mayhem.

A stolen diamond from Antwerp sets off a chain of spectacularly bad decisions across London's criminal underworld. Turkish and Tommy — small-time boxing promoters — get tangled up with ruthless gangster Brick Top when their fighter goes missing. Meanwhile, a group of inept thieves steal the diamond from under the nose of Boris the Blade, a thoroughly unreliable Russian.

Add in Bullet Tooth Tony, the most terrifying debt collector in London; Franky Four Fingers, a compulsive gambler who can't stop long enough to deliver the stone; and Mickey O'Neil, a bareknuckle boxing Irish pikey who may or may not be throwing fights — and you have a perfect storm of organised chaos.

Multiple storylines collide at breakneck speed, edited with razor-sharp precision and soaked in Ritchie's signature style: freeze-frames, rewinds, ironic voiceover, and dialogue so quotable it still lives rent-free in the collective British consciousness 25 years on.

The Threads

Five storylines.
One diamond.

01

Turkish & Tommy

London boxing promoters in way over their heads with Brick Top after their fighter bites the dust. They hire Mickey the pikey to replace him — a decision that goes immediately sideways.

02

Franky Four Fingers & the Diamond

Benicio del Toro's Franky steals an 84-carat diamond in Antwerp and is supposed to deliver it to New York jeweller Avi. He can't stop gambling long enough to make the handoff.

03

Boris the Blade

"Boris the Blade. Boris the Bullet-Dodger." He wants the diamond for himself, hires the incompetent Cousin Avi gang to steal it, and keeps ending up shot. Doesn't die. Keeps coming back.

04

Avi & Bullet Tooth Tony

Dennis Farina as the exasperated New York jeweller flying back and forth to London. Enlists the services of Bullet Tooth Tony — a man so hard he has a bullet embedded in his chest as jewellery.

05

Mickey O'Neil

Brad Pitt in the performance of his career. A traveller who fights bareknuckle for fun, speaks entirely unintelligible Gaelic-tinged English, and whose mother's caravan becomes the fulcrum around which the entire plot pivots.

The Verdict
10
Out of 10 — Perfect

Twenty-five years on and Snatch remains untouchable. The editing is masterful, the performances are electric, and the screenplay is airtight — every thread pulled tight at exactly the right moment. Brad Pitt is otherworldly. Alan Ford as Brick Top is one of cinema's great villains. Guy Ritchie never made anything better. One of the finest British films ever put to celluloid.
