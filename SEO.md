## 1. SEO Philosophy

After 23 years, the core principles haven't changed as much as people think. Here's what matters:

**Be unique. Be fun. Be useful. Be engaging. Be accurate.**

That's it. If every page you build lives up to those five words, you're going to rank. Every tactic, every technical optimisation, every piece of content should serve that goal.

### Engagement is everything

One of the most important ranking signals is **engagement score**. Google is watching how users interact with your page:

- **Keep bounce rate low** — if users land and leave, Google notices
- **Keep session-to-search high** — users who land on your page and then use your site's search are highly engaged
- **Time on page matters** — the longer someone stays, the more relevant Google considers your content

### The CTR signal

Here's something most people don't realise: if you're sitting in **4th position** and you're getting **more clicks than the site in 3rd**, that's a massive relevancy signal to Google. Your title tag and meta description are doing their job, and Google will reward you for it. This is why crafting compelling titles and descriptions isn't just about keywords — it's about earning the click.

### The bottom line

> Focus on the brand and the rankings will come.

Good quality content that is unique and accurate continually performs well across multiple algorithm updates. If you're chasing algorithms, you're always one step behind. If you're building a brand that users trust, you're future-proof.

## 2. Keyword Research

Every page starts here. Before you write a single word of content, you need to understand what people are searching for.

### The process

1. **Open Google Keyword Planner or SEMrush**
2. **Pull all relevant related keywords** for the topic your landing page will target
3. **Gather at least 5 keyword variations** — you need breadth to understand how people search for this topic
4. **Identify the head term** — this is usually the most popular term by search volume. This becomes your primary keyword
5. The remaining variations become your secondary and tertiary keywords, which you'll weave into the title, meta description, and content

### Example

For a Gatwick parking page:
- **Head term:** gatwick parking (highest volume)
- **Variations:** gatwick airport parking, parking at gatwick, gatwick car park, cheap gatwick parking, park and ride gatwick

The head term drives your URL, H1, and title tag. The variations get worked into the meta description, subheadings, and body content naturally.

## 3. Topic Research

Once you have your keywords, you need to find **10 to 20 topics** that relate to the search terms. These topics become the backbone of your content — each one gets its own paragraph and heading on the page.

### How to find topics

- Look at what the top-ranking pages cover
- Check Google's "People Also Ask" section
- Review related searches at the bottom of Google results
- Use SEMrush's topic research tool
- Think about what a customer would genuinely want to know

### Why 10-20 topics?

This ensures your page has **depth and authority** on the subject. Google and LLMs both reward comprehensive content that covers a topic thoroughly. A thin page with 200 words won't compete against a page that answers every question a user might have.

## 4. On-Page Foundations

These are the non-negotiable elements that every page must get right.

### URL Structure

The page URL must contain the main keyword.

- **Good:** `/gatwick-parking.html`
- **Bad:** `/page-12345.html` or `/travel/uk/airports/parking/gatwick/overview`

Keep it clean, short, and keyword-rich.

### Title Tag

**Rules:**
- Main keyword at the **start** of the title
- Follow with an inciting sales message
- Maximum **75 characters** total

**Formula:** `[Main Keyword] - [Compelling Sales Message]`

**Example:** `Gatwick Parking - Compare & Save Up to 60% | Holiday Extras`

The title tag is your first impression in the SERPs. It needs to earn the click.

### Meta Description

**Rules:**
- Main keyword **close to the start**
- You have **150 characters** to explain what the page is about
- Merge your 2nd or 3rd keyword variation naturally into the description

**Example:** `Compare Gatwick airport parking from just £X/day. Park and ride, meet & greet, and on-airport options. Free cancellation on all bookings.`

The meta description doesn't directly impact rankings, but it massively impacts **click-through rate** — and as covered in the philosophy section, CTR is a ranking signal.

## 5. Page Structure & Content Hierarchy

This is the blueprint for building a landing page that ranks. Every element is deliberate, in this exact order.

### 5.1 Anchor Links

At the very top of the content, include **anchor links** to key sections on the page. These serve two purposes:
- Google uses them as **sitelinks in the SERPs**, giving you more real estate on the results page
- Users can jump directly to the content they need

### 5.2 Breadcrumbs

The first structural element on the page should be a **breadcrumb** with **schema markup** around it. This helps Google understand your site hierarchy and displays the breadcrumb trail in search results.

### 5.3 H1 Heading

Your **H1 tag must contain the main keyword**. There should be only one H1 per page. This is the most important heading on the page and tells both Google and users exactly what the page is about.

### 5.4 Leading Sub-heading (Anti-Bounce Element)

Immediately below the H1, include a sub-heading with your **leading message**. This is critical for preventing bounces. The user has clicked through from Google — you need to immediately confirm they're in the right place.

This sub-heading should include one or more of:
- **Price** (e.g., "From just £X.XX per day")
- **Main sales message** (e.g., "Save up to 60%")
- **Number of products** (e.g., "Compare 12 Gatwick car parks")
- Something directly relevant to the meta title and description

The goal: **match the promise you made in the SERP listing**. If your title says "Compare & Save", the first thing they see on the page must reinforce that.

### 5.5 USP Paragraph

The next paragraph should highlight your **unique selling propositions**. Get the user excited about the product and your brand:

- Flextras (if applicable)
- Free cancellation
- Any exclusive benefits or deals
- Anything that makes the user want to search or read more

This paragraph is your pitch. Make it compelling.

### 5.6 Topic Paragraphs (The Core Content)

This is the meat of the page. Take the **10 to 20 topics** you researched earlier and dedicate **one paragraph with a relevant heading** to each.

**Requirements for each topic paragraph:**
- **Factual and accurate** — no fluff, no filler
- **Up to date** — outdated information kills trust and rankings
- **Detailed** — cover the topic thoroughly in each paragraph
- **Relevant heading** — an H2 or H3 that clearly describes what the paragraph covers
- **Internal links** — link as many relevant words as possible to other pages on your site that cover that topic in more detail

### 5.7 Product Tables

Include a **product comparison table** listing relevant products with links to individual product pages. For example, a Gatwick parking page should list all Gatwick car parks with:

- Car park name (linked to product page)
- Lead price
- Key features

See [holidayextras.com/gatwick-airport-parking.html](https://www.holidayextras.com/gatwick-airport-parking.html) as a reference.

Product tables serve double duty: they're excellent for **internal linking** and they show lead prices to **entice users to search**.

### 5.8 Comparison Content

**Comparison content is critical for LLMs.** AI models love structured comparisons because they can easily parse and reference them. Include:

- **Comparison tables** (e.g., meet & greet vs park & ride)
- Feature-by-feature breakdowns
- Price comparisons

Format these as HTML tables where possible — LLMs parse tables far better than prose.

### 5.9 Bullet-Pointed Content

Break key information into **bullet points**. This helps both:
- **Users** — easy to scan and digest
- **LLMs** — structured content is easier to parse and cite

Use bullets for tips, feature lists, requirements, and any content that benefits from a list format.

### 5.10 Customer Reviews

Customer reviews from your own platform and from **Trustpilot** are hugely valuable. They add:

- **Relevancy** — real user experiences about the product
- **Stickiness** — users stay longer reading reviews
- **Trust signals** — for both users and search engines

**Each review should link to a page with more reviews about that specific product.** This creates a natural internal linking structure and encourages deeper site engagement.

### 5.11 FAQs

FAQs are one of the most valuable sections on any landing page.

**How to build your FAQ section:**
1. Take your **top 5 target keywords**
2. Search each one on Google
3. Pull every question from the **"People Also Ask"** section
4. List them with the **exact question and answer** as they appear
5. Aim for a **minimum of 20 FAQs per page**

Answer each question fully and concisely. A complete answer in a single paragraph is ideal — this is exactly the format that LLMs and AI Overviews pull from.

**Add FAQ schema markup** (JSON-LD) to this section so questions can appear directly in search results.

## 6. Internal Linking & Site Architecture

Internal linking is one of the most powerful and underused SEO levers.

### Content-Level Linking

Within every paragraph on your page, **link as many relevant words as possible to other pages on your site**. This helps Google:
- Discover and index related content
- Understand the relationship between your pages
- Distribute authority throughout your site

Don't be shy with internal links. If a word or phrase relates to another page you have, link it.

### Header & Footer Links

Your site header and footer should contain links to your **most competitive pages** — the ones targeting your highest-value keywords.

**Include:** Pages targeting competitive head terms (e.g., `gatwick-parking.html`)

**Exclude:** Lower-priority comparison or long-tail pages (e.g., `gatwick-meet-and-greet-vs-park-and-ride.html`)

Header and footer links appear on every page of your site, so they carry significant internal link equity. Reserve them for the pages that need it most.

### Product Tables as Linking Hubs

Product comparison tables naturally function as internal linking hubs. Every product name links to its product page, creating a web of relevant internal links that Google loves.

## 7. Content Quality Principles

Content quality is the single biggest factor in long-term SEO success. Algorithm updates come and go, but **good quality, unique, and accurate content continually performs well**.

### The rules

- **Unique** — never duplicate content from other sites or your own pages
- **Accurate** — every fact, figure, and claim must be correct
- **Factual** — back up claims with evidence and real data
- **Up to date** — review and refresh content regularly; outdated content loses rankings
- **Engaging** — write for humans first, search engines second
- **Well-formatted** — clean headers, nicely formatted paragraphs, logical structure
- **High reading standard** — content should read to a high quality standard; no typos, no lazy writing

### Tips and advice

Every page should include **useful tips and practical advice** about the products. Talk about the products in detail — how they work, what to expect, how to make the experience as easy as possible for the customer. This builds trust and keeps users on the page longer.

## 8. Image Optimisation

Every image on the page must be optimised. No exceptions.

- **Filename:** Give the image a relevant, descriptive filename (e.g., `gatwick-parking-terminal-entrance.jpg`, not `IMG_4523.jpg`)
- **Alt tag:** Every image must have a relevant alt tag that describes what the image shows
- **Optimised:** Images must be optimised for both mobile and desktop — compressed for fast loading without sacrificing quality
- **Original:** Use your own images wherever possible. Stock photos are a last resort
- **Responsive:** Ensure images display correctly on all screen sizes

## 9. Technical SEO Checklist

Every page must pass this checklist before going live.

### Required elements

- [ ] **Schema markup** in JSON-LD format — use [schema.org](https://schema.org) as your reference. Tag everything that can be tagged: reviews, breadcrumbs, FAQs, products, organisation, etc.
- [ ] **Canonical tag** — every page must have a self-referencing canonical tag
- [ ] **HTML lang tag** — specify the language of the page (e.g., `en-GB`)
- [ ] **robots.txt** — ensure the page is accessible to search engine crawlers
- [ ] **Favicons** — proper favicon set for all devices
- [ ] **Open for Googlebot** — no accidental noindex or disallow directives
- [ ] **Mobile responsive** — page works seamlessly on mobile and desktop
- [ ] **EAAT compliant** — demonstrate Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness

### Performance

- [ ] **PageSpeed score above 90** on [PageSpeed Insights](https://pagespeed.web.dev/) — must be in the green before pushing live
- [ ] Test on both mobile and desktop

### Post-launch

- [ ] **Submit via Google Search Console** after every update — don't wait for Google to find changes, tell them

## 10. Brand & Identity

Focusing on the brand and all the things that make us a brand are critically important. Every page, every piece of content should reinforce who we are.

**Tell Google, LLMs, and our users:**
- **Who we are** — our history, our expertise, our credentials
- **Why we're here** — our mission, what we offer, why we exist
- **What makes us different** — our USPs, our values, our customer promise

Brand signals are becoming increasingly important as Google and LLMs prioritise trusted, established sources. A strong brand presence across your content isn't just good marketing — it's good SEO.

## 11. LLM & AI Overview Optimisation

LLMs are here to stay, and **ranking in AI Overviews (AIO) is the next SEO**. We need to craft content that ranks in both traditional search and LLM-powered results.

### How to optimise for LLMs

- **Schema markup** — LLMs parse structured data more effectively than unstructured content
- **Comparison tables** — AI models love structured comparisons they can reference and cite
- **Bullet points** — structured lists are easier for LLMs to parse and extract
- **Answer questions fully in one paragraph** — LLMs pull concise, complete answers. If a question can be answered in a single paragraph, do it
- **FAQ sections** — direct question-and-answer format is exactly what AI models are trained to extract

### The shift

Traditional SEO optimises for Google's crawlers and ranking algorithms. LLM optimisation is about making your content **the most citable, structured, and authoritative source** on a topic. Both matter. Build pages that serve both.

## 12. Compliance

### ASA Requirements

Any prices mentioned on the page **must have an asterisk** with a note stating:
- The **date** the price was pulled
- The **location/route** the price applies to

This is a legal requirement from the Advertising Standards Authority. Don't skip it.

## 13. Common Mistakes

After 23 years, these are the mistakes I see again and again.

### Don't do cloaking

Showing different content to search engines and users is a guaranteed way to get penalised. Google has been catching this since the early 2000s. Don't even think about it.

### Don't do obvious link building

If your link building strategy is obvious, it's probably a bad strategy. Bought links, link farms, and spammy guest posts will hurt you in the long run. Build links naturally through great content and genuine relationships.

### Don't over-SEO

Don't spam a landing page with keywords. Everything on the page needs to be **relevant and useful to the customer**. If it doesn't serve the user, it doesn't belong on the page. Over-optimisation is a ranking killer.

### Focus on the brand

> The rankings will come if you focus on the brand.

Stop chasing algorithm updates. Stop trying to game the system. Build a brand that users trust, create content that genuinely helps people, and the rankings will follow.

## 14. Key Metrics

These are the metrics that matter. Track them religiously.

| Metric | Why it matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Sessions** | Overall traffic — are people finding the page? |
| **Session to Search** | Users who land and then search — a strong engagement signal |
| **Search to Book** | Conversion from search to booking — revenue indicator |
| **Conversion Rate** | End-to-end conversion — the ultimate measure of page effectiveness |
| **Bounce Rate** | Users who leave immediately — high bounce = something is wrong |
| **Time on Page** | How long users engage with your content — longer is better |

### The engagement equation

Low bounce rate + high session-to-search + good time on page = **strong engagement score**. In a competitive market, engagement is what separates page 1 from page 2.
